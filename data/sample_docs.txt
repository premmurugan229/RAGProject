Diabetes, stroke, and epilepsy are interconnected, with diabetes acting as a major risk factor for both cerebrovascular accidents (stroke) and an increased susceptibility to epilepsy. [1, 2]  
Diabetes and Stroke 

• Increased Risk: Diabetes doubles to quadruples the risk of stroke, with individuals being 2–6 times more susceptible to ischemic stroke. 
• Mechanism: High blood sugar (hyperglycemia) damages blood vessels, leading to vascular endothelial dysfunction, increased arterial stiffness, and systemic inflammation. 
• Stroke Types: Patients with diabetes are especially susceptible to small vessel diseases (lacunar infarcts) and have a higher prevalence of subcortical ischemic infarction. 
• Outcomes: Acute hyperglycemia during a stroke is associated with poorer clinical outcomes, higher mortality rates, and increased brain damage. 
• Management: Strict control of blood glucose (target 140–180 mg/dL in acute settings), blood pressure (&lt;140/90 or lower), and lipid levels are critical for prevention. [1, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7]  

Diabetes and Epilepsy 

• Seizure Causes: Both high (hyperglycemia) and low (hypoglycemia) blood sugar can trigger seizures. 
• Non-ketotic Hyperglycemia (NKH): A rare complication of uncontrolled diabetes where blood sugar &gt;200 mg/dL causes seizures. These are often focal motor seizures. 
• Connection: Diabetes can cause brain lesions or vascular damage that make an individual more prone to developing epilepsy. 
• Treatment: Seizures related to NKH are often best treated by correcting the hyperglycemia (fluids and insulin) rather than relying solely on antiepileptic drugs. [2, 8, 9, 10, 11]  

Key Takeaways for Management 

• Prevention: Managing blood pressure, controlling cholesterol, and controlling diabetes are key to preventing both stroke and related neurological complications. 
• Medication: Some diabetes medications, like pioglitazone, have shown promise in reducing the risk of a second stroke in patients with insulin resistance. 
• Emergency Recognition: Symptoms of diabetes-related stroke are the same as general stroke symptoms (numbness, confusion, trouble walking) and require immediate medical attention. [1, 3]
